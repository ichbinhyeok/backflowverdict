# 12 Vendor Setup Package Lock

## 1) Why this document exists
This document locks the current setup-led paid layer so future agents do not misframe it as:
- a generic branding package
- a full vendor SaaS
- or a vague consulting offer with no clear scope boundary

The setup package should stay narrow, sellable, and directly connected to the free handoff wedge.

## 2) Locked one-line definition
BackflowPath setup aligns one small busy vendor office's annual-notice and failed-test customer-brief workflow so that office can send customer-ready links or PDFs in its own style without rewriting the same explanation every time.

## 3) What setup is actually selling
Setup is not selling software installation.

Setup is selling:
- office alignment
- sender defaults
- repeat wording defaults
- utility-specific sample briefs
- a ready-to-send starting state for one office

The strongest plain-language version is:

"We set up your customer brief workflow so your office can send annual notice and failed-test explanations in your own company style right away."

## 4) What setup is not
The setup package is not:
- a full vendor portal
- a CRM
- a billing system
- a team-permission product
- an official submission platform replacement
- a white-label platform by default
- a broad custom design engagement

If the package starts drifting into those categories, it is no longer a setup offer. It becomes a different product.

## 5) Relationship to the free wedge
The free wedge should already be strong enough for one real send.

Setup exists because a vendor office may want:
- its own company line
- its own default wording
- sample briefs for the utilities it handles most
- a cleaner PDF and sender presentation
- a faster office starting point

Setup should not depend on weakening the free wedge.
The right setup need is alignment and convenience, not artificial withholding.

## 6) Locked target buyer
The current best buyer for setup is:
- a small but busy backflow-related office
- usually owner plus admin, coordinator, or CSR
- repeated annual notice and failed-test volume
- enough customer explanation pain to care about consistency
- not yet demanding a full back-office platform replacement

The first working buyer is usually not the field technician.
It is the office person who keeps sending explanations, links, screenshots, and follow-up notes.

## 7) What the setup package should include
The current locked setup scope is:
- one office
- company name, phone, email, and sender line alignment
- one annual-notice default brief template
- one failed-test default brief template
- PDF header and customer-facing wording cleanup
- utility-specific sample briefs for about 1 to 3 recurring utilities
- one revision round
- one short onboarding step such as a call or recorded walkthrough

## 8) What setup should explicitly exclude
To keep the offer sharp, setup should exclude:
- unlimited utilities
- unlimited revisions
- all issue types
- team seats and permissions
- CRM or customer database behavior
- automatic email or SMS sending
- utility portal integrations
- utility submission system replacement
- full white-label by default
- multi-branch or multi-brand rollout

## 9) Setup deliverables that make an office feel "ready"
The package should produce a visible "our office can use this now" state.

Recommended deliverables:
- one office starter path for annual notices
- one office starter path for failed tests
- aligned sender defaults
- 2 to 3 sample customer briefs
- sample PDF output
- one quick-start guide for the office

The point is not presentation polish alone.
The point is that the office feels operationally ready on day one.

## 10) Intake items to request from the vendor office
The intake should stay short and directly tied to the output.

Current recommended intake items:
1. company name
2. main office phone
3. main office email
4. preferred sender name or sender line
5. the 1 to 3 utilities the office wants first
6. one recent annual notice example or pasted notice text
7. one recent failed-test example or pasted failed-result text
8. any wording the office always wants included for next-step instructions
9. one review contact for approval
10. whether the office wants a short call or a recorded walkthrough

This is enough to set up the workflow without turning the package into a discovery project.

## 11) Scope boundary for revisions and onboarding
The setup package should include:
- one consolidated review pass
- one revision round
- one short onboarding moment

It should not include:
- repeated training for multiple staff members
- open-ended copywriting
- utility research expansion beyond the agreed starter utilities
- repeated visual redesign passes

## 12) Current packaging guidance
The offer should be framed as:

"customer brief workflow setup"

It should not be framed as:
- branding package
- PDF generator customization
- vendor SaaS onboarding

The commercial reason is simple:
vendors care less about having a logo on a tool than about not having to explain the same annual-notice or failed-test situation from scratch every time.

## 13) Current pricing posture
The current public pricing posture is now locked more tightly.

Public pilot price:
- $149 one-time

Pricing rules:
- a single visible setup price is better than multiple public tiers
- the public price should stay attached to the narrow setup scope
- recurring should not be bundled into setup
- the first price increase can happen only after a few paid setups prove the package is repeatable

The current public packaging rule is:
- one offer
- one clear scope
- one visible price

The current tactical reading is:
$149 one-time is appropriate as a pilot office-setup price, but it should be treated as a pilot price, not as a permanent ceiling.

## 14) Current sales-operation lock
The setup package should now be sold through a simple email-first manual flow.

Locked operating flow:
1. vendor sees the setup offer and emails in
2. BackflowPath confirms basic fit
3. vendor sends the short intake
4. payment happens before setup work starts
5. first draft is delivered within 3 business days after payment and intake are complete
6. one revision round closes the setup

This means the product does not yet need:
- a self-serve checkout
- automated onboarding
- a team workspace
- a subscription flow

## 15) Recurring is intentionally deferred
Recurring should remain off the public offer for now.

Reason:
- the current strongest value is initial office alignment
- repeat usage has not yet been proven enough to justify a public subscription promise
- adding recurring too early will blur the setup message

Recurring should only be revisited after repeated office use creates real demand for:
- stored defaults
- reusable templates
- history
- regeneration
- continuity across repeated jobs

## 16) Decision rules for future agents
- Do not reframe setup as generic branding work.
- Do not expand setup into pseudo-consulting with no scope boundary.
- Keep setup tied to annual notice and failed test until real paid demand proves a wider surface.
- Keep setup co-branded and vendor-first unless the recovery-loop strategy changes.
- Treat office readiness, not feature breadth, as the outcome being sold.
- Keep the public setup offer at one clear pilot price unless real sales data justifies a change.
- Keep recurring off the public offer until repeat-office demand is real.
